01 — The Real Context

Two Transformations.
One Moment.

The insurance industry is being structurally reshaped. AI-driven underwriting, embedded insurance distributed through non-traditional channels, digital-first competitors with no legacy infrastructure, and platform models that remove the intermediary entirely — these are not future risks. They are present realities that are already redrawing the competitive map. Hollard's response to that disruption is what makes the People Strategy role genuinely consequential. This is not a conversation about HC in isolation. It is a conversation about whether Hollard builds the organisational capability — the leadership agility, the workforce adaptability, the cultural resilience — to compete and win in a fundamentally different industry.

The People Strategy agenda at Hollard is therefore dual: it must support the transformation of the HC function itself — deepening its strategic influence and analytical capability — while simultaneously serving as a primary enabler of the broader business transformation Hollard needs to execute. These are not sequential. They must happen together, with the same urgency.

02 — How I Think About People Strategy

Outside-In.
Evidence Before Prescription.

The most common failure mode in people strategy is starting from within the HC function — designing solutions and hoping the business notices. The work that creates lasting value starts from the outside: with business strategy, competitive context, and what the organisation actually needs its people to be capable of doing differently.

// PRINCIPLE 01
Business context before HC design

A people strategy that isn't rooted in what the business is trying to become is a people programme. The first obligation is to understand Hollard's strategic direction deeply enough to make HC a genuine contributor to business outcomes — anticipating what the organisation needs before it needs to ask.

HC functions create their greatest value when they move the conversation from "what does HR deliver?" to "what does this business need its people to be capable of?" The sequence matters.
// PRINCIPLE 02
Diagnose before designing

Organisations that have been through significant change carry complexity that isn't visible from the outside. Before designing anything, I invest time understanding what's actually happening: what leaders believe is true, what employees experience as real, and where the gap between intention and reality actually sits.

The presenting problem is rarely the real problem. Listening at depth — before forming recommendations — is itself a strategic act, and one that builds the credibility that makes everything else possible.
// PRINCIPLE 03
Co-design over imposition

People strategies that are built with leaders — not for them — have a fundamentally different adoption profile. The HC Strategy executive brings rigour, challenge and provocation; the organisation brings context, legitimacy and commitment. Neither works without the other, and the best strategies are genuinely co-authored.

The organisations with the most effective HC functions treat people strategy as a shared leadership responsibility — not an HC deliverable that leaders are asked to endorse.
// PRINCIPLE 04
Iterative commitment, not big-bang delivery

Transformation portfolios that are sequenced, piloted and adjusted consistently outperform those built on comprehensive upfront design. I commit to hypotheses early and to scaled delivery only when evidence supports it — building credibility through demonstrated value on short cycles rather than through the ambition of the plan alone.

Quick wins build trust. Trust enables the harder work. The goal is not to have the most impressive strategy at Month 1. It is to have the most durable one at Year 3.
// PRINCIPLE 05
Analytics that change conversations

People analytics has its greatest impact when it changes the quality of the decision-making conversation — not when it replaces it. I build data capability around the decisions leaders are already trying to make, not around producing reports that demonstrate the HC function is active.

The measure of a mature people analytics function is not how much data it produces. It is how many better decisions it enables — and whether leaders reach for it when facing their hardest calls.
// PRINCIPLE 06
Culture is a leadership behaviour, not an HC programme

The most durable cultures are ones where leaders own them through daily decisions — not ones where HC runs the engagement process and presents the results. The role of HC is to design the conditions, name the expectations and hold the mirror. Then step back and let leadership lead.

Culture change begins and ends with what leaders model, tolerate and reward. Everything else — the frameworks, the academies, the values work — is supporting infrastructure for that accountability.
On the thinking behind the thinking: Two bodies of work meaningfully shape how I approach this role. The idea that HC creates its greatest value when it starts from the business context outward — not from HR process inward — is a principle I've seen validated across every organisation I've worked with. It fundamentally changes what gets prioritised, how HC earns credibility, and how quickly it moves from support function to strategic partner. Separately, the research on what distinguishes high-impact HC functions from average ones consistently points to the same factors: how embedded HC is in business decision-making, how much leaders genuinely own people outcomes, and how well the function anticipates capability needs before the business names them. Neither insight is particularly controversial — but both are surprisingly rare in practice.

04 — How I'd Work

Discover First.
Design Together. Deliver with Evidence.

I'm not arriving with a pre-built strategy. I'm arriving with a structured way of discovering what Hollard's strategy should be — shaped by your leadership priorities, your organisation's lived experience, and the evidence that emerges from genuine diagnostic work before any significant commitment is made.

What I commit to on arrival

Deep listening. Structured inquiry. An honest diagnostic of where the HC function is, what the organisation needs it to become, and what the distance between those two things actually requires. I'll form hypotheses — not conclusions — and share them openly so the CPO and Exco can test them against their own insight. Collaboration from the first conversation.

What I hold back until I've earned the right to recommend

A fixed 12-month plan. A pre-determined target operating model. A culture architecture designed before I've spent time in the organisation. The most durable people strategies I've built all started with patience about recommendations and urgency about understanding. That discipline is what I bring — and it is what I believe Hollard deserves.
